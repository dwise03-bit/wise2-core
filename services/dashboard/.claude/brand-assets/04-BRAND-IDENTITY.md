# WISE² CORE — VISUAL IDENTITY BRIEF

*Visual Language for a Production-Ready Platform Built by Hustlers*

---

## 01 — IDENTITY STRATEGY STATEMENT

Wise² Core's visual identity must communicate reliability, ambition, and accessibility in equal measure. This is not a playful brand or a premium luxury brand—it's a builder's brand. The visual identity should feel grounded, confident, and honest. Every design decision should speak to the founder's reality: clarity matters more than ornamentation, speed matters, and trust is earned through specificity, not decoration. The identity should be modern enough to feel current, but solid enough to convey the dependability of production infrastructure. Think less "venture-backed startup" and more "battle-tested system." The visual system should work beautifully on a founder's phone, on a server terminal, and on a billboard.

---

## 02 — LOGO DIRECTION

**Primary Direction**
Wordmark + optional secondary symbol. The name "WISE² CORE" should be the primary mark. The "²" (superscript 2) is a visual differentiator and should remain integral to the logo. Consider a paired secondary mark (symbol) that can be used at small scales or in isolation, but the full wordmark is the primary expression.

**Character**
The logo should feel:
- Purposeful, not decorative
- Solid, not trendy
- Forward-thinking but grounded
- Accessible (works at any size, any medium)
- Professional without being corporate

**Style Notes**
- **Geometric foundation** — constructed, not hand-drawn. Precision matters.
- **Modern sans-serif base** — clean, clear, no serifs. Think technical precision.
- **The "²" is a subtle visual signature** — it should feel intentional, not like a typo. It's the only distinctive graphic element.
- **Minimal and tight** — tight letter spacing, no unnecessary flourishes. Every pixel earns its place.
- **Works in black + white, at small scales, and in motion** — it should be as readable on a favicon as on a website hero.

**What to Avoid**
- Rounded, friendly, approachable sans-serifs (feels too casual for infrastructure)
- Serif fonts (too formal, too "corporate law firm")
- Playful letterforms or alternates
- Heavy drop shadows, gradients, or 3D effects (feels dated)
- Anything that looks "startup-y" or trendy
- Over-designed secondary marks or mascots
- Decorative elements or icons integrated into the wordmark

**3 Logo References**

1. **Stripe** — The mark uses geometric precision, modern sans-serif, tight spacing, and minimal color. Borrow the confidence and simplicity. Stripe's logo feels like a company that has figured something out. Wise² Core should feel the same way.

2. **Mailchimp (old identity)** — Clean wordmark, distinctive without being decorative, works at all scales, geometric but warm. The mark conveys reliability through simplicity.

3. **Figma** — Bright, bold, geometric, confident. The mark is iconic but based on clear structure. Borrow the boldness and the sense that this is a platform built by people who understand design.

## 03 — COLOR PALETTE

**Primary Color: Dark Navy / Charcoal + Bright Action Orange**

The primary color should be a confident dark (navy or charcoal, hex: #1A1F36 or similar) paired with a bright, energetic secondary accent (action orange, hex: #FF6B35 or similar).

**Rationale:**
- Dark navy conveys reliability, stability, and technical competence (infrastructure lives in the background)
- Bright orange is the action color—it appears in CTAs, highlights, and moments that demand attention (the founder's ambition)
- Together, they create contrast without feeling playful or uncertain
- Both colors work on dark backgrounds (important for technical interfaces) and light backgrounds (important for marketing)

**Secondary Colors**

1. **Neutral Gray** (hex: #6B7280) — for supporting text, disabled states, subtle hierarchy
2. **Light Background** (hex: #F9FAFB or #FFFFFF) — for clean, open interfaces
3. **Accent Green** (hex: #10B981, optional) — for success states, positive confirmations ("deployment successful")
4. **Alert Red** (hex: #EF4444, optional) — for errors or critical alerts

**Color Personality**
This palette is grounded and functional. It's not trendy or playful. The dark navy + bright orange combination creates high contrast and legibility (important for technical information) while the neutral grays and subtle accents keep interfaces clean and focused. The overall feeling is "built by people who know what they're doing."

**Usage Rules**
- Navy is the primary, appears in logos, headers, and key interface elements
- Orange is accent-only—use sparingly for CTAs, highlights, and moments that matter
- Use full-saturation orange for primary CTAs; consider slightly muted orange for secondary actions
- Grays are for supporting information, not primary communication
- Green/red are for status states only (avoid in primary branding)
- Ensure sufficient contrast for accessibility (dark text on light backgrounds, light text on dark backgrounds)

**Palette Type**
Triadic + high contrast. Primary (navy) + accent (orange) + supporting (neutral grays).

## 04 — TYPOGRAPHY

**Primary Typeface: Inter or Roboto**

Both are modern, geometric sans-serifs that feel contemporary without being trendy. Inter is slightly more refined and contemporary; Roboto is more universally installed. Either works.

**Character:**
- Neutral, readable, trustworthy
- Geometric construction (circles, squares) rather than humanistic
- Works beautifully at small sizes (important for UI)
- Has clear distinction between similar characters (1/l/I, 0/O)
- Open character set for global languages

**Usage Hierarchy**

| Context | Typeface | Weight | Size | Notes |
|---------|----------|--------|------|-------|
| **Hero Headlines** | Inter | Bold (700) | 48–64px | Clear, confident, memorable |
| **Section Headers** | Inter | Semibold (600) | 28–36px | Visual hierarchy without ornamentation |
| **Subheaders** | Inter | Semibold (600) | 18–24px | Secondary information |
| **Body Copy** | Inter | Regular (400) | 16px+ | Highly readable; adequate leading |
| **UI Labels / Buttons** | Inter | Semibold (600) | 14–16px | Clear, tappable targets |
| **Code / Technical** | JetBrains Mono | Regular (400) | 12–14px | Monospace for precision |

**Secondary Typeface: JetBrains Mono (for code and technical content)**

Code snippets and technical documentation need monospace. JetBrains Mono is readable, distinctive, and feels modern. It's commonly used in developer tools, which keeps Wise² Core feeling native to the developer/founder experience.

**Avoid**
- Serif fonts (feels formal, contradicts the brand)
- Hand-drawn or decorative fonts (feels unprofessional for infrastructure)
- Ultra-light or ultra-thin weights (hard to read, especially at small scales)
- Multiple typeface families (stick to Inter + JetBrains Mono)
- All-caps body text (reduces readability)

## 05 — IMAGERY & PHOTOGRAPHY STYLE

**Overall Aesthetic**
Real, honest, grounded. Not aspirational or staged. This is documentary-style photography of actual founders, products, and infrastructure—not models or stock photos. The aesthetic should feel like "this is really happening," not "this is what we want you to imagine."

**Subject Matter**
- Founders at work (at desks, on laptops, focused)
- Screenshots of actual dashboards and monitoring
- Real infrastructure (servers, terminals, deployment logs)
- Technical workspaces (not polished offices, but functional ones)
- Proof of work (code being written, deployments happening, monitoring alerts)
- Community moments (founders helping other founders, discussing problems)

Avoid:
- Models or staged scenarios
- Unrealistic "everyone's so happy" scenarios
- Stock photo aesthetics (too polished, too posed)
- Lifestyle imagery (founders with yoga mats, sunrise yoga, beach work)
- Anything that feels like venture capital marketing

**Color Treatment**
- Natural, minimal color grading
- Slightly warm white balance (not clinical or cold)
- High contrast (legible on all devices)
- Muted in some cases (to show technical depth without being harsh)
- Allow navy and orange to appear naturally in the environment when possible (founder's setup, branded swag, etc.)

**Composition**
- Close, human-scale (founder perspective, not bird's-eye)
- Authentic moments (not perfectly composed)
- Focus on hands, faces, details (not wide establishing shots)
- Show the work, not just the result

## 06 — ICONOGRAPHY & ILLUSTRATION

**Style**
Line-based icons with consistent stroke weight. Not filled, not overly detailed, not illustrative. These should feel like UI elements, not decorative graphics.

**Weight**
- 2px stroke for desktop/web
- Consistent across all icons
- Line-end caps: rounded (not sharp)

**Personality**
Functional and utilitarian. Icons should communicate instantly what they represent (deploy, monitor, scale, backup). They should feel like they belong in a technical interface, not in a children's book.

**What to Avoid**
- Rounded, friendly, "approachable" icon styles
- Filled, heavy icons
- Decorative or illustrative icons
- Emoji-like expressions or personalities
- Overly complex icon designs

## 07 — DESIGN PRINCIPLES

**Principle 1: Clarity Over Decoration**
Every visual element should serve a purpose. Founders don't have time for design flourishes. The visual system should prioritize information hierarchy and readability over visual interest. A well-designed dashboard is beautiful because it's clear, not because it's ornate.

**Principle 2: Consistency at Scale**
The identity should work at a favicon (16px), on a business card (2" x 3.5"), on a website hero (full viewport), and on a billboard. Every element of the system (logo, color, type, icons, spacing) should feel cohesive whether you're seeing it on a phone or a large display. This is a builder's brand—we don't compromise on detail.

**Principle 3: Honest and Grounded**
No gradients, no blur effects, no trendy 3D. The visual system should feel substantial and real. Think "infrastructure" not "startup." The brand is built on honest communication, and the design should reflect that. Surfaces are flat. Colors are solid. Typography is clear.

**Principle 4: Functional Beauty**
Beauty comes from functionality, not decoration. A well-spaced interface is beautiful. A clear typographic hierarchy is beautiful. Consistent spacing and alignment are beautiful. The visual system should prioritize utility—and let beauty emerge from that utility.

## 08 — BRAND EXPRESSIONS

**Social Media Profiles & Posts**
- Logo as profile picture (squared up, dark background)
- Navy background or clean white header image
- Orange accent in header or profile banner
- Bio: Clear, benefit-focused, no corporate jargon
- Post style: Mix of product screenshots, founder stories, educational snippets, community highlights

**Website & Web App**
- Hero: Dark navy or white background, bold typography, orange CTAs
- Supporting sections: Alternating white/light gray backgrounds
- Dashboard: Light backgrounds, navy headers, orange accents, high contrast
- Clear visual hierarchy and generous spacing

**Business Cards & Stationery**
- Front: Logo + minimal contact info, white/light gray background
- Back: Orange bar or stripe (optional)
- Typeface: Inter only
- No decorative elements
- Substantial cardstock

**Presentations & Pitch Decks**
- Slide template: White or navy backgrounds
- Headlines: Bold Inter, navy or orange
- Orange accent bars for emphasis
- Real product screenshots (not mockups)
- Consistent margins and spacing
- Minimal transitions

## SUMMARY

Wise² Core's visual identity is grounded, confident, and functional. It feels like infrastructure—solid, reliable, built by people who know what they're doing. The system is modern without being trendy, professional without being corporate, and approachable without being cutesy. Every design choice reflects the brand's commitment to clarity, reliability, and honesty.

When a founder sees Wise² Core's visual identity, they should think: "These people have thought this through. I can trust them with my infrastructure."
